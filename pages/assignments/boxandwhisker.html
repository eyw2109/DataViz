The Box-and-Whisker Plot

Also known as the box plot, box-and-whisker diagram, or Tukey boxplot, the Box-and-Whisker plot is a graphical method of showing the spread of data on a graph. This method was first introduced by John Tukey (1915-2000) in the book Exploratory Data Analysis in 1977 to enourage the exploration of different ways of looking at data, and to promote new data analysis techniques. The Box-and-Whisker plot is primarily useful in revealing the central traits and variables through a five-number summary based on the minimum, first quartile, median, third quartile, and maximum of a data set.


In the image above, the median, first quartile, and third quartile, form a rectangular box. The segment between the third quartile and maximum, and the first quartile and minimum are the whiskers. The IQR shows the interquartile range.

Ocassionally, there are outliers and suspected outliers:


Outliers are 3 times or more above or below the third and first quartile respectively. Suspected outliers are half that of the outlier. Similarly, the outer-fence is 3 times that of the IQR (3 x IQR) and the inner-fence is 1.5 times that of the IQR (1.5 x IQR).

Here are some benefits and detriments of the Box-and-Whisker plot






Advantages

  • Box plots can summarize large amounts of data
  • Box plots display the range and distribution along a number line
  • Box plots show outliers
  • Box plots can indicate a data's symmetry and skew-ness

Disadvantages

  • The original data is not clearly shown
  • The mean cannot be identified
  • The mode cannot be identified
  • Box plots can only be used with numerical data

From the example above, we see that the x-axis are the data-set(s), and the y-axis is the height in feet. For the "heights of 50 states," the median height is somewhere just below 5,000ft, for the "heights of 219 volcanos," the height is in the 6,000ft range. The maximum and minimum appear to be near Wyoming and Louisana, and Tupungatito and Anak Kraktau respectively.

One can also see the outliers, particularly in the "height of 50 states" graph. California, Colorado, Washington, and Hawaii are suspected outliers within the inner-fence near the maximum, Delware and Florida are suspected outliers near the minimum. Alaska is the lone outlier with a height of at least 20,000ft in the outer-fence. This shows that the majority of the heights of the states are between 2,000 and 11,000ft, while Alaska and many of the other suspected outliers have skewed the data.
